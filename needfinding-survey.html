Needfinding and Methodology

Survey

Our needfinding began with a survey shared to various Facebook groups including the HCI Spring 2019 group and the Class Year 2019, 2020, and 2021 groups. We expect, based on the groups the survey was sent to, that most if not all respondents are University of Rochester students. Our goals for the survey were to get a gauge of what students thought about their own stress levels, coping methods they found valuable, and their opinion on journaling and our project idea.

Seventy-five survey responses were recorded. Results are detailed below.

Q1: What methods do you use to cope with stress?

In this question, users could check all answers that applied. Shown in the graph are the options we provided.

Users could also provide their own answers. Some mentioned include:
  • Medication
  • Sex/masturbation
  • Media/recreation (video games, movies, reading)
  • Art (painting, drawing)
  • Eating/indulging
  • Planning, organizing, making lists
  • Working

Q2: How would you rank your stress levels over the last two weeks?

Q3: How well-equipped are you at dealing with stress?

Q4: How would you rank your access to mental health resources (e.g. counseling or any other methods you might wish to take advantage of)?

Q5: What are the biggest barriers to students using mental health resources?

Q6: How beneficial is journaling when dealing with problems such as stress, anxiety, or depression?

Notes on Results

  • A large majority (90%) of students reported their stress levels as "moderate" or higher, with none reporting "very low".
  • About half of students reported feeling moderately equipped to deal with stress, with only 19% feeling more confident.
  • A majority (63%) of respondents felt that their access to mental health resources such as counseling was at least adequate.
  • The greatest barrier reported to students using mental health resources was lack of time, followed by lack of access, stigma, and financial concerns.

Q7: Would you use this product? Why or why not?

Question 7 was a free-response answer. The graph summarizes the results.

Of the users that responded they would not or probably would not use the product, by far the most popular reason was privacy concerns. Others include:
  • They don't find journaling helpful or aren't in the habit of doing it
  • They journal for themselves and not to get feedback, or don't think automated feedback would be useful
  • They find physically writing down their thoughts to be an important part of journaling

Of the respondents who said they would try the product, most seemed to already believe that journaling could be a helpful tool and were interested in the feedback aspect, particularly that feedback could be received from the comfort of home.

Q8: Which of these features would be beneficial to you?

In Question 8, users could select as many of the given responses as they felt relevant.

The "Recommendations" option refers to a feature where if the application noticed a user reporting they were happier on days they exercised, for example, it would notify the user of this correlation and recommend exercise.

All of the options are features we are planning to implement in the project (whether we are able to TBD), but it was valuable to see what features students considered most helpful.
